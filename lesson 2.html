Lesson 1:

  • Introduction to LunaSim: Modeling Motion
  • Subject/Course: Physics
  • Grade Level: 9-12
  • Estimated Time: 45 minutes
  • Learning Objectives:
  • Students will understand how system dynamics models represent motion (etc)
  • Skills: Students will use LunaSim to build a basic motion model and analyze its behavior.
  • Materials:
  • Computers with LunaSim
  • Projector
  • Basic motion worksheet
  • Procedure:
  • Part 1: Introduction (10 minutes)
  • Hook: Real-world motion example (e.g., a falling object).
  • Introduce LunaSim: Explain stocks, flows, and basic modeling.
  • Guided LunaSim tutorial: Students create a simple model (e.g., constant velocity).
  • Part 2: Activity (25 minutes)
  • Motion problem: Students model specific motion scenarios (e.g., distance vs. time).
  • Data collection: Students use LunaSim to gather data.
  • Analysis: Students analyze how changing parameters (e.g. distance vs. time) affect the model
  • Part 3: Wrap-up (10 minutes)
  • Class discussion: Students share findings
  • Quick assessment: Short worksheet or Q&A to check understanding
  • Homework: Review LunaSim; begin a simple physics modeling project using Euler's method

Lesson 2

  • Simulating Accelerated Motion with Euler's Method in LunaSim
  • Subject/Course: Physics
  • Grade Level: 9-12
  • Estimated Time: 45 minutes
  • Learning Objectives:
  • Students will be able to simulate and analyze uniformly accelerated motion using Euler's method in LunaSim
  • Skills: Students will construct a velocity-dependent motion model with acceleration as a constant flow and compare its output to uniform acceleration
  • Materials:
  • Computer with LunaSim
  • Projector
  • Accelerated motion worksheet
  • Procedure:
  • Part 1: Introduction (10 minutes)
  • Hook: Prompt discussion: "How could we simulate gravity's effect on a falling object without solving equations by hand?”
  • Introduce the concept of numerical modeling and Euler's method, and how it applies in LunaSim
  • Model walk through: constant acceleration -> increasing velocity -> changing position
  • Part 2: Activity (25 minutes)
  • Students build a LunaSim model with three variables: position as a stock, a flow into position representing motion, and velocity and acceleration as variables
  • Define the flow using the velocity variable, and use influence arrows to show acceleration affecting velocity, and velocity affecting the flow
  • Run simulations with different dts (Δt = 1s, 0.1s, 0.01s, etc) and graph outputs
  • Explore model stability, error, and what happens when Δt gets bigger
  • Part 3: Closing (10 minutes)
  • Class discussion: Students share findings and discuss questions like:
  • When does Δt get more accurate?
  • What could cause numerical errors in models like this?
  • Quick assessment: Short multiple-choice / short-answer response on Euler's method and interpreting model output
  • Homework: Review LunaSim, continue project, and modify the model to simulate a thrown object with an initial upward velocity

Lesson 3

  • Modeling Forces and Nonlinear Motion in Lunasim
  • Subject/Course: Physics
  • Grade Level: 9-12
  • Estimated Time: 45 minutes
  • Learning Objectives:
  • Students will be able to model nonlinear motion by incorporating variable forces like air resistance and spring force into LunaSim
  • Skills: Students will use LunaSim to build and analyze a dynamic system where acceleration depends on current velocity or position, applying Newton's Second Law in a computational environment
  • Materials:
  • Computer with LunaSim
  • Projector
  • Nonlinear motion worksheet (scenarios: drag, spring, projectile, etc.)
  • Procedure:
  • Part 1: Introduction (10 minutes)
  • Hook: “Why doesn't a real object keep accelerating forever when dropped from a height?” - show a quick video of a skydiver or a bouncing spring
  • Review Newton's Second Law and introduce nonlinear forces like:
  • Air resistance: depends on velocity
  • Spring force: depends on position
  • Demonstrate how to represent these forces using variables and influence arrows in LunaSim
  • Part 2: Activity (25 minutes)
  • Students choose one system to model:
  1. Free-falling object with air resistance (drag)
  2. Vertical spring-mass oscillator
  3. Simple projectile motion (1D, vertical)
  • In all models:
  • Position = stock
  • Flow into position = velocity (variable)
  • Acceleration = variable determined by net force/mass
  • Drag modeled as a velocity dependent force
  • Spring modeled as a position dependent force
  • Students will create the appropriate influence arrows, simulate the model under different conditions, and graph position, velocity, and acceleration over time
  • Part 3: Closing (10 minutes)
  • Class discussion: What behaviors were unexpected? How is nonlinear motion different from constant acceleration?
  • Quick assessment: Students complete a short reflection or exit ticket explaining how one variable influenced acceleration in their model
  • Homework: Review LunaSim, continue and wrap up project, write a short summary of how your system behaved, and include at least one screenshot of your graph
